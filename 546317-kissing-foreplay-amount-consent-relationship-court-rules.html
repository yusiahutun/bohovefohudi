    South African Court of Appeal has issued a judgment that has clarified several things relating to sexual consent.

    According to a report done by the Centre of Legal Studies (CALS), a landmark ruling done by the court emphasised that kissing does not equate to consent for sex, affirming that "no" means "no."

    The court underscored that consent, even if initially granted for a particular sexual act, can be retracted during the act itself.

    The report stated that in October 2021, a South African High Court acquitted Loyiso Coko, who had been convicted of sexually assaulting his ex-partner by a regional court.

    While initially sentenced to seven years in prison, Coko successfully appealed his conviction to the high court.

    The report further stated that the complainant had made several pleas to her ex-partner to stop but to no avail.

    The Incident happened in 2018 when the accused and the complainant who was 20 years old at the time, were involved romantically.

    Despite this, the court concluded that the accused lacked intentions of sexually assaulting his ex-partner, thus acquitting him of sexual assault charges.

    The court reportedly used various myths related to sexual assault to justify the accused's perspective.

    This included citing their engagement in other forms of intimacy, such as kissing and oral sex, as indicative of consent.

    Court of Appeal changes verdict

    The court's verdict, however, caused outrage among community members, causing the public prosecutions to take the matter to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

    Various organisations came together and served as friends of the court where they shared their insights on the ruling.

    The court recently upheld the appeal against Coko, reinstating his guilty verdict. The SCA ruled that consent to "foreplay" does not equate to consent for intercourse.

    Judge Petse emphasized that being in a relationship with the accused does not imply automatic consent to all sexual acts.

    She underscored that consent for one specific sexual act should not be construed as a blanket authorisation for all other acts.
