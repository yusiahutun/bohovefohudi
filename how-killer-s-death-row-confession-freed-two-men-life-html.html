    Strapped to a gurney in Oklahoma's execution chamber, with a fatal cocktail of drugs about to flow into his veins, Michael Lee Wilson declared: 'Malcom Scott and De'marchoe Carpenter are innocent.'

    At the time of Wilson's 2014 execution, Carpenter and Scott had already spent 20 years in prison for the murder that Wilson committed when they were 17.

    In the early morning hours of September 10, 1994, 19-year-old Karen Lashawn Summers, the mother of an infant, was gunned down outside a house party in Tulsa, Oklahoma in a drive-by shooting sparked by rivalries between Bloods and Crips gang members.

    Carpenter and Scott, now both 40, were convicted of the murder as teens despite no physical evidence linking them to the crime, on the strength of two eyewitnesses who later recanted their testimony.

    Now the two wrongfully convicted men have spoken out in interviews with the Daily Beast, for an exhaustive report on their decades-long saga. 

    'Every day is like a birthday for me,' Carpenter told the website one year after their release. 'I don't have a dollar in my pocket but look at me. Working, trying to survive and enjoying life.'

    Wrongfully Convicted: De'marchoe Carpenter and Malcom Scott were freed after 22 years in prison for murder when a man on death row confessed to the slaying

    In 2014, Michael Lee Wilson was in Oklahoma's execution chamber, seen in this file photo, when he declared that Carpenter and Scott were innocent

    It took two more years following Wilson's death-row confession for Carpenter and Scott to walk free from prison, ending their decades in prison with the help of a dogged private investigator and the testimony of a killer.

    The two wrongfully convicted men were no angels, to be sure.

    Growing up during the crack cocaine epidemic in hardscrabble North Tulsa, both teens were admitted members of the Red Mob Gangster set of the Bloods, according to police. (Carpenter now denies he was in a gang.)

    In November of 1993, the then 16-year-olds were themselves both struck by bullets in a gangland shooting that injured nine, when a man with an assault rifle shot up the Wagon Wheel bar.

    A year later, they came under suspicion for the murder of Summers.

    Summers had recently arrived at the house party on the 200 block of E. 29th Street North, where members of the Crips gang were swarming on the front lawn, when around 2.45am bullets flew into the crowd from a passing maroon sedan. 

    Michael Wilson was interviewed by police detectives a day later, when they found him with a Lorcin .380 handgun, the same caliber as the murder weapon, and a maroon Ford Taurus.

    But incredibly, he was never charged in the murder, and walked free.

    Just four months later, Wilson killed his co-worker at a convenience store, 30-year-old Richard Yoast, in a brutal baseball-bat attack.

    Wilson was sent to death row for that murder, and was convicted with accomplices Richard Harjo and Billy Alverson. 

    The same trio carried out the shooting that killed Summers at the house party, Wilson later confessed.

    Killed: Convenience store worker Richard Yoast was beaten to death in the 1995 murder that sent Wilson to death row

    But cops zeroed in on Carpenter and Scott as suspects in the Summers murder.

    Without a murder weapon or forensic evidence that the pair were involved in the shooting, prosecutors relied on the testimony of two witnesses: Rashun 'Rawbeanie' Williams, who returned fire on the shooters with a .22 pistol, and Kenneth Price, a 107 Hoover Crip who was shot in the buttocks by the killers.

    Both men later recanted their testimony in sworn affidavits obtained by private investigator Eric Cullens, who worked for years to prove Carpenter and Scott's innocence.

    'For sixteen years, I have lived with a guilty conscience,' Price said in the affidavit obtained by the Daily Beast, adding that he had been pressured by Tulsa police into testifying against Carpenter and Scott.

    'I got shot in the buttocks so it is obvious that my back was toward the action,' Williams said in his affidavit. 'I initially told officers I didn't see anything and I didn't know who did the shooting.'

    The witnesses allege that police threatened to charge them with the shooting unless they testified against Carpenter and Scott.

    True Killers: Piru Street Blood members (left to right) Michael Lee Wilson, Richard Harjo, and Billy Alverson were Summers' true killers, Wilson confessed. The three men were all convicted in a separate homicide several months after the drive-by shooting that killed Summers

    Prosecutors also pressured Carpenter and Scott to testify against one another, and cut a deal to reduce their sentences. But both men refused to point the finger at the other.

    'I'm not going to take somebody down that I know did not do it,' Scott told The Daily Beast. 'This is a friend to me. This is a guy that I know got my back.'

    Carpenter and Scott were convicted of the murder and sentenced to life plus 175 years in prison.

    Neither gave up trying to prove their innocence, with Carpenter recording video pleas from behind bars. 

    'I'm in prison for a murder I didn't do,' Carpenter pleaded in one of the videos. 'I've been in here, trapped like an animal in a cage for a crime I didn't do.

    Dogged Gumshoe: Private investigator Eric Cullen obtained the witness affidavits that helped free Carpenter and Scott

    'Someone please help me.'

    Cullen, the private investigator, teamed up with attorneys from the Oklahoma Innocence Project, who were struck by the case after reading trial transcripts.

    The attorneys learned that Wilson was willing to admit to the murder, but his attorneys wouldn't let him swear to his additional crime until his death penalty appeals in the murder of Yost had been exhausted.

    Finally, on January, 7 2014, two days before he was to be executed, Wilson taped a sworn statement confessing to the murder of Summers. 

    'Mike Huff and another detective, they're asking me all these questions. But I'm nervous because I know they got the murder weapon,' Wilson told an Oklahoma Innocence Project attorney in the video. 

    'So it kind of blew me away that I got caught with the gun and they just let me go. They didn't arrest me for possession of a firearm or anything... They just let me go. So to this day, I don't know how my nameâ€¦ got brought to the police's attention, but all I know is I had a murder weapon on me and they let me go.'

    Wilson said police found him with the murder weapon, a Lorcin .380 semi-auto pistol like the one seen in this file photo, but he was never charged in Summers' murder

    Two years later, in May of 2016, Carpenter and Scott walked out of prison as free men in high spirits.

    'It's obvious that I can't pursue my NBA career but I've been told that I have Denzel Washington and George Clooney's good looks. I think I'm going to be an actor,' Carpenter joked to reporters gathered outside the prison.

    Since his release, Carpenter has worked nine different jobs, and now works as a caregiver at Brookhaven Hospital, where he assists patients with traumatic brain injury. 

    Scott works as a machine operator in a metal processing plant an hour southeast of Tulsa.

    The former prosecutor who thought he'd put the pair away for life isn't convinced of their innocence, though. 

    'Look, man, you're dealing with all gang members in a gang shooting in a gang war,' former Assistant District Attorney Mark Collier told the Daily Beast. 

     'All these people know each other. They're in rival gangs that hate each other. So who did it?' Collier said. 

    'The only evidence is Malcolm Scott and De'Marchoe Carpenter. All evidence points to them.' Wilson, he added, had 'about the same credibility as Charles Manson.'
