    Conjoined twinning is among the most fascinating human malformations in human history. Some twins undergo successful separation depending on the part conjoined and shared organs. However, some, like Chang and Eng Bunker, remain together forever. Commonly referred to as the Siamese twins, the duo used their medical disorders to their advantage, thus achieving a lot in life.

    Chang and Eng Bunker were conjoined twin brothers born in Thailand, formerly known as Siam. The duo was joined by a short, flexible band of cartilage and flesh at the sternum. Their livers were connected. Regardless, they lived normally, a life characterized by marriage and children.

    Who were Chang and Eng Bunker?

    Chang and Eng Bunker were Siamese-American conjoined twin brothers. The duo was displayed as curiosities becoming the most studied human being in the 19th century. They were born on 11th May 1811 in Samut Songkhram, Rattanakosin Kingdom.

    The duo was discovered in 1824 by Robert Hunter, a Scottish businessman. After sensing their potential to make money, he convinced them to relocate to the USA. However, it took five years to get the two out of Siam since the king forbade their departure.

    Eventually, they arrived in the USA in 1829 at 17, where they made a name and fortune for themselves by touring different states. They performed physical feats such as swimming and somersaults during their early shows. However, they took control of their actions after touring the British Isles, learning English, and ending the initial deal with Hunter.

    Working independently, they started engaging audiences in a formal parlour setting, talking and answering questions. Finally, they gained American citizenship and settled in North Carolina. More than 15 years after leaving Siam allegedly as enslaved people, the duo bought their slave plantation.

    In 1850, more than half of Chang and Eng Bunker’s slaves were children below seven years. These kids grew up to be sold for profit or worked on the Bunker’s plantation fields. The press described the Bunker’s treatment of enslaved people as harsh, but the duo constantly denied the accusations.

    Enslaving people and supporting the Confederacy during the Civil War caused a sordid stain on their lives. This had a negative effect on how their audiences started viewing them. Regardless of the cold welcome, they continued their tours after the war.

    Chang and Eng Bunker insisted on touring due to their financial necessities and losing their slaves during the war. The two presented themselves as poor older men with huge families to support. In addition to touring the United States, the duo tried their luck in Europe, making trips in:

    • Britain

    Chang and Eng Bunker’s wives and children

    The twins started pursuing the daughters of David Yates, a neighbouring farmer. Luckily, several years later, Chang and Adelaide, the slimmer and more attractive sister, had fallen head over heels in love. But, unfortunately, the other duo, Eng and Sarah, hadn’t fallen in love. Sarah took five years to agree to the twin’s entreaties.

    Both couples, Chang and Adelaide and Eng and Sarah were married by a Baptist preacher in David Yate’s living room. The ceremony took place in 1843. After the union, the foursome returned to Chez Bunker, where their marital bed had been enlarged and strengthened to accommodate them all.

    The four slept together on the reinforced marital bed. The conjoined brother would sleep in the middle and their wives on their sides. Chang and Eng Bunker’s children were born after their union was officiated.

    The duo conceived 21 children. Chang and Adelaide had ten babies, while Eng and Sarah had 11. The foursome had twelve daughters and nine sons. Unfortunately, two children were deaf, while another two succumbed to burning before three years. However, none of the 21 kids were born as twins.

    Even though it is unclear how the four made babies, it is alleged that the first children were born just six days apart in 1844, and another pair, born later, were separated by eight days. After showcasing amazing forbearance over the years, the wives finally started arguing.

    Chang and Eng set up a separate home for their wives to create harmony and end the fights. Adelaide and her ten children were placed in one home, while Sarah and her 11 were placed in the other. The two wives also agreed to split each week between their twin husbands.

    Chang and Eng Bunker’s death

    Chang and Eng Bunker remained in good health for the better part of their lives. However, Chang became numb on the right side after a stroke in 1870. Eventually, his right leg needed to be kept in a sling, but Eng remained supportive. Unfortunately, he became a heavy drinker remaining in poor health condition.

    In January 1874, Chang contracted bronchitis. As a result, the family doctor recommended he stays indoors and warm. On 15th January, as their norm, the Bunkers travelled through the cold weather to Eng’s house, worsening Chang’s health.

    Chang seemed to have recovered for the following few days but couldn’t breathe comfortably at night. He passed away on 17th January 1987. Chang and Eng Bunker died at 62 at Mount Airy.

    What was Chang and Eng Bunker’s cause of death? An autopsy revealed that Chang died of cerebral blood clots while Eng died of fright. Eng died less than three hours after his twin. Others claim that Eng died of blood loss as his circulatory system was incomplete since his brother’s connecting bands didn’t circulate blood in return.
