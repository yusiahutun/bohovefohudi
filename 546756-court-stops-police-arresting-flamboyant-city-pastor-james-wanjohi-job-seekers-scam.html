    Flamboyant Nairobi businessman and controversial preacher James Wanjohi has obtained orders restraining police officers from arresting him.

    Who owns Worth Start Africa?

    The police are seeking Wanjohi, the founder of Worth Start Africa, for allegedly masterminding a job seekers' con scheme.

    Wanjohi, accused of conning job seekers of about KSh 600 million, obtained the restraining orders on Tuesday, April 30.

    The orders bar police officers from arresting him until a case he filed in court is heard and determined.

    Wanjohi, who has vehemently dismissed the allegations of conning job seekers, and pastoring a church from which many of the con scheme victims fellowship, also got relief after Justice Diana Kavedza granted him an anticipatory bail of KSh 100,000.

    The order reads, "The applicant is admitted to anticipatory bail in the sum of KSh 100,000 to be deposited in court."

    Can DPP charge James Wanjohi?

    The court, however, clarified that the police were at liberty to press charges against him.

    "To avoid doubt, the respondents are at liberty to investigate or charge the applicant for any criminal conduct. However, they shall not arrest or detain him in view of orders above or until further orders of the court," Kavedza clarified.

    The court issued the restraining orders after Wanjohi's lawyer, Danstan Omari, told the court that the Directorate of Criminal Investigations (DCI) had instilled "unjustified "fear in his client, yet he is a law-abiding citizen.

    He added that the constant harassment by DCI detectives had caused him huge embarrassment before his staff.

    "That officers from the first respondent have instilled unjustified fear in the applicant due to erroneous reports to the authorities by the applicant's competitors who claim to work with the National Employment Authority.     Police raided Wanjohi's recruitment agency's offices at Pension Towers in Nairobi and carted away computers and other documents for further investigation.

    This was after several Kenyans, mostly members of the Jesus Culture Ministry in Roysambu, accused Wanjohi of conning them money claiming that he would help them secure jobs abroad.

    However, they suspected things were not okay when they all received a template letter informing them that their visa applications had been declined.

    Wanjohi, in a statement to the public on Tuesday, April 30, rebuffed the accusations, saying that he had neither conned any job seekers nor was he a pastor at Jesus Culture Ministry.

    He claimed his business and political rivals had orchestrated the allegations to dim his 2027 ambition of becoming Roysambu MP.
